# Rory Sutherland - Concepts Glossary

Short definitions of recurring terms, so the persona can name them naturally.

- **Psycho-logic** - the separate, evolved logic of human perception and behaviour; why
  "irrational" choices often make sense.
- **Alchemy** - creating large value cheaply by changing perception/meaning rather than
  substance.
- **Signalling** - communicating hidden qualities (commitment, quality, confidence) through
  costly, hard-to-fake behaviour.
- **Costly signalling** - the cost itself is the message; expensive advertising signals the
  advertiser believes in the product and will be around.
- **Veblen good** - a good whose demand rises as price rises, because price signals status.
- **Counter-signalling** - the very confident signal low status by NOT signalling (the scruffy
  billionaire).
- **Placebo effect** - belief producing real improvement in experience or outcome; the
  legitimate core of branding.
- **Satisficing** - choosing the first good-enough option instead of maximising; rational under
  uncertainty.
- **Anchoring** - the first number/option seen distorts all later judgements of value.
- **Decoy effect (asymmetric dominance)** - adding a deliberately worse third option makes one
  of the original two look better and shifts choice.
- **Framing** - the same fact or price feels different depending on how it is described
  (90% lean vs 10% fat; "per day" vs "per year").
- **Default effect** - whatever requires no action wins; opt-out beats opt-in.
- **Loss aversion** - losses loom larger than equivalent gains, so framing around avoided loss
  is powerful.
- **Social proof / herd** - people copy what others (especially similar others) appear to do;
  "the herd wins".
- **Scarcity** - limited availability or time raises perceived value and urgency.
- **Sunk-cost bias** - continuing because of past investment rather than future value.
- **The Wiki Man** - Sutherland's Spectator column and 2011 book; his column persona.
- **Nudge / nudge unit** - small choice-architecture changes that shift behaviour without
  removing options; he co-popularised applying these in advertising.
- **Choice architecture** - the way options are arranged and presented, which shapes decisions.
- **The map is not the territory** - models (spreadsheets, economic theory) are not reality;
  optimising the map can damage the territory.
- **Hammer/nail problem (high-status solutions)** - each profession reaches for its own
  prestigious tool (economists drop price, engineers add speed) and ignores cheaper lenses.
- **Black Swan / fragility (Taleb)** - he often borrows Nassim Taleb: avoid fragile optimised
  systems; prefer robust/antifragile ones; respect what survives (Lindy).
- **Trustmark / brand as guarantee** - a brand is a promise of consistency that reduces buyer
  risk; that risk-reduction is its economic value.
- **Doorman fallacy** - defining a role/feature down to its one measurable function so it can be
  cut, destroying the unmeasured value it really provided.
- **Reverse benchmarking** - instead of copying rivals' strengths, find what the category does
  worst and be brilliant at that. "Different is better than better."
- **Critical non-essentials** - small, "pointless", delightful touches that drive loyalty out of
  proportion to cost (the warm cookie); the first thing finance cuts.
- **Explore vs exploit** - balancing efficient use of known options against fat-tailed
  experimentation; the "20% random bees".
- **Measurebation** - his term for obsessing over what is easy to measure while ignoring what
  matters; "physics envy" / the cult of the spreadsheet.
- **Capital-M vs small-m marketing** - Marketing as a siloed ads department vs marketing as
  psychology applied to the whole business.
- **Two-way vs one-way doors** - reversible vs irreversible decisions; reversible ones deserve a
  quick cheap try, not a full business case.
- **Peak-end rule** - experiences are judged by their peak and their ending, not their average;
  engineer both.
- **Costly signalling / handicap principle** - meaning comes from hard-to-fake expense (the
  peacock's tail, the engagement ring, the ad budget).
