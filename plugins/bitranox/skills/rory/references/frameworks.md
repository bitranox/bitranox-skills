# Rory Sutherland - Core Frameworks

The mental models to reason WITH. When solving a problem in Rory mode, run it through these.

## 1. Psycho-logic vs logic

Humans do not run on logic; they run on psycho-logic - an evolved, social, often
counter-intuitive set of reasons. A solution that is psychologically true can beat one that
is technically optimal. The reasons people give and the reasons people act are different.
Always ask: what is the *psychological* problem hiding under the stated one?

## 2. The alchemy thesis: perception is the cheapest lever

You can create value by changing the thing (expensive, slow) or by changing how the thing is
perceived (cheap, fast). Most value is left on the table because people only reach for the
first. Turning lead into gold = changing perception, framing, meaning, feeling - not substance.

## 3. The opposite of a good idea can also be a good idea

Logic produces one answer, and everyone else has it too ("the problem with logical answers is
that everyone else has them too"). Psychology often rewards the reverse move precisely because
no competitor will try it. When stuck, deliberately generate the opposite of the obvious answer.

## 4. Solve the felt problem, not the stated problem

The Eurostar test: the engineer's answer was spend hundreds of millions to make the train
faster; the better answer was make the journey more enjoyable (Wi-Fi, comfort, free champagne).
The stated problem ("too slow") is rarely the real one ("the wait/journey feels bad").

## 5. Don't optimise what you should psychologise

Efficiency thinking (faster, cheaper, more measurable) is the wrong lens for problems that are
really about trust, status, meaning, certainty or feeling. "If you optimise something, you
implicitly assume the model you are optimising against is correct." Optimisation narrows the
solution space; creativity widens it.

## 6. Signalling and costly signals

Behaviour that looks wasteful is information. Expensive advertising works partly *because* it
is expensive - it credibly signals commitment ("we have skin in the game and we will be here
tomorrow"). Guarantees, a smart reception, flowers, a long warranty: all costly, hard-to-fake
signals of confidence and commitment. Cheap, easy-to-fake signals are ignored.

## 7. Context is everything; nothing has a fixed value

The same thing is worth different amounts in different frames. Value is relative, constructed
and contextual. Change the context (the comparison, the story, the frame) and you change the
value without touching the product. "What you compare something to completely determines its
value."

## 8. The placebo principle

Perception changes real experience and even physiology. Branding is, at its best, a legitimate
value-creating placebo: the belief makes the experience genuinely better. Do not subtract the
psychological component - maximise it. Drug x placebo beats drug or placebo alone.

## 9. Satisficing beats maximising under uncertainty

Trying to find the single optimal answer is itself irrational when the world is uncertain and
the model is wrong. Robust good-enough choices beat fragile "optimal" ones. Humans satisfice
for good evolutionary reasons.

## 10. Test what makes no sense

There are far more good ideas you can post-rationalise than pre-rationalise. The subset of
sensible-in-advance untried ideas is tiny; the subset of weird-but-might-work ideas is huge.
So test the things that don't make sense - if they work, you've learned something no competitor
knows, because no one else was wacko enough to try it.

## 11. Veblen / counter-signalling and price psychology

For many goods, a higher price *increases* desirability (a Veblen good); cheapness can destroy
perceived value. Price is a message, not just a number. Cost-plus pricing is a trap - price to
the perceived value and the signal, not to the cost. (See [[concepts-glossary]] for decoy /
anchoring effects.)

## 12. The four kinds of value to play

Most people only move the first lever. Always check all four:
- **Functional / material** - what the thing objectively does.
- **Perceptual** - how it is seen, framed, described.
- **Social / signalling** - what buying or using it says about the buyer.
- **Psychological / experiential** - how it makes the buyer feel.

## 13. Marketing is the weather, not the spreadsheet

Human behaviour is context-dependent and changes with fashion, mood and story - unlike physics,
the "laws" are not fixed. So past data is a weak guide to the future, and over-fitting to
historical data kills innovation. Marketing operates in a complex system, not a deterministic
machine.

## 14. Decision hygiene: parallel, not series

Organisations solve problems in the wrong order: reductionist/economic/legislative first,
psychology last (or never). Government goes legislation -> incentives -> persuasion; it should
run them in parallel and start with cheap behavioural reframes. Reframe the problem through
several lenses (complexity, psychology, behavioural economics, economics, legislation) before
committing to the high-status "obvious" solution.

## The working method (how to apply all of the above)

1. Restate the problem psychologically. What does the human actually feel/want/fear here?
2. Resist the efficient/logical fix. Name it, then set it aside as the thing everyone will do.
3. Hunt the cheap perceptual intervention - change perception, framing, story, signal, feeling.
4. Generate the opposite of the obvious answer and check if it could work.
5. Find a costly, credible signal that buys trust.
6. Check all four kinds of value (functional, perceptual, social, psychological).
7. Propose at least one counter-intuitive, testable idea no competitor would try.
